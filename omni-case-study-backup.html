CONTEXT

Why this project mattered

Benefits administration is one of the most universally dreaded employee experiences. Omni's users — employees navigating health insurance, retirement contributions, and medical claims — were forced to move between five separate systems: a retirement app, an insurance app, PDF benefit cards, a claims portal, and email threads. Each transition meant re-authentication, context-switching, and confusion.

The result was a wave of avoidable support tickets, eroded trust, and employees simply giving up on the benefits they had earned.

"The goal was not to add features. It was to reduce the cognitive load of accessing benefits people already had."

RESEARCH

What users were telling us

I synthesized findings across three sources: app store reviews, user interviews, and support ticket logs. Four patterns repeated consistently enough to become our design compass.

71%

Coverage information was inaccessible

Users could not locate basic plan details without leaving the app to search PDFs or external portals. This was a top driver of support volume.

78%

A low bar would create high loyalty

Users said that simply surfacing their cards, coverage summary, and claim status in one place would make them recommend the platform.

40%

Simplicity directly increased trust

When we removed unnecessary steps and exposed progress clearly, trust scores improved significantly in usability testing.

priority_high Key Finding

Claims were the biggest pain point

Paper-heavy, error-prone, with no status visibility. Users described submitting claims as "stressful and uncertain."

05

Design Process

How I approached the redesign

Rather than treating this as a feature-by-feature rebuild, I reframed the problem around the full benefits lifecycle: from understanding coverage to getting reimbursed. Each design decision was evaluated against that lens.

01

Consolidated information architecture

I merged the retirement and insurance mental models into a single navigation hierarchy. The challenge was reconciling two product teams' taxonomies without making either group's users feel lost. I ran card-sorting sessions to validate the final structure before building.

02

Claims workflow redesign: 3-step guided flow

The old claims process required users to download PDFs, fill them out manually, and email or fax them in. I replaced it with a guided in-app flow: Upload, Classify, then Review and Submit. I chose a stepped model over a single long form because research showed users were abandoning mid-way due to overwhelm. The new flow also added inline validation to catch errors before submission.

03

Digital benefit cards and Apple Wallet

Lost or inaccessible benefit cards were the single highest-volume support ticket category. I designed a digital card system with tap-to-show coverage details, and integrated Apple Wallet so users could access their card at a medical visit without opening the app. This was the highest-impact, lowest-complexity decision of the project.

04

Unified design system

With four previously separate experiences, there was no shared component language. I built a modular system with accessible color contrast ratios, consistent spacing, larger touch targets, and voiceover labels. The system needed to work for older employees (a major user segment) as well as digitally native users.

Reflection

What I learned

The most important insight from Omni was that enterprise UX failures are almost never caused by missing features. They are caused by poor sense-making: interfaces that force users to hold too much context in their heads at once.

What surprised me was how much outsized impact came from small decisions. Using plain language instead of benefits jargon, surfacing claim status proactively instead of making users search for it, showing a progress indicator in the claims flow instead of leaving users wondering if their submission worked.

Omni reinforced a principle I now carry into every project: the goal of any complex system should be to become invisible. When users can focus on the task and not on navigating the interface, that is when the design is working.
